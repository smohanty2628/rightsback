01 Where to find your dates

Two dates drive your termination eligibility calculation: your copyright registration date and your release date. If you don't know either one — don't worry. Our calculator can find both for you automatically.

🔍 Easiest option — let us find it for you

On the song details step, type your song title and click Search →. Our system searches our database of 4.5 million+ U.S. Copyright Office records and returns the registration number, registration date, and release year automatically — no manual lookup needed.

If the search doesn't find your song, or if you want to verify the information yourself, here's where to look:

Finding your USCO copyright registration number

1
Go to Publicrecords.copyright.gov This is the U.S. Copyright Office's free public search. It covers musical works registered from the 1970s onward.
2
Select "Title" from the search type dropdown Type your song title exactly as it appears on the original recording or sheet music. Try different spellings if needed.
3
Look for records with type "PA" or "PAu" "PA" means Performing Arts — this is the category for musical compositions. The registration number will look like PA 1-234-567 or PAu 3-000-000.
4
Note the registration date and claimant name The date the copyright was registered and the original claimant (publisher or songwriter) are both shown in the record. Copy these into the Rights Back® form.
⚠ Important: registration date ≠ grant date

The copyright registration date is not the same as the date you signed your publishing deal. Your publishing contract (grant/transfer date) is usually earlier. If you know when you signed the contract, enter that date in the Grant/transfer date field — it's the most accurate starting point for the calculation.

Finding your song's release date

1
Check AllMusic — allmusic.com Search for the album or single. AllMusic has detailed release histories for recordings going back to the 1950s, including original release dates and re-issues.
2
Check Discogs — discogs.com Discogs is a user-maintained music database with vinyl, CD, and digital release dates. It's especially strong for singles and B-sides from the 1960s–1990s.
3
Check Billboard archives — billboard.com/charts/year-end If your song charted, the Billboard archives will show the chart year, which gives you a reliable release year even if you don't have the exact date.
4
Check the album liner notes or original contract Physical copies of the original album often include a copyright year on the back cover (e.g., © 1978). Your original contract or royalty statements may also list the recording date.

02 Music Business Glossary

Click any term to expand the explanation. These cover the concepts behind your eligibility check.

When you write a song, you own the "publishing rights" — the right to reproduce the song, license it for movies, TV, and ads, and collect royalties when it's performed publicly or streamed. Most songwriters in the 1970s–1990s signed publishing deals that transferred these rights to a music publisher for a set number of years — or permanently. The publisher collected all the income the song generated and paid the songwriter a percentage (typically 50%). This is the right that the Copyright Termination Act allows you to reclaim.
Every commercially released song has two separate copyrights: 1. The Master Recording — the actual recorded performance (the specific studio recording on vinyl, CD, or streaming). Typically owned by the record label. 2. The Publishing / Composition — the underlying song itself: the melody, lyrics, and structure. This is owned by the songwriter or their publisher. Rights Back® focuses on publishing rights. Even if a label owns the master recording of your song, you (or your heirs) may be able to reclaim the publishing copyright through termination.
A copyright assignment is a contract where you formally transfer ownership of your copyright — or a portion of it — to another party (usually a publisher or label). Most publishing contracts from the 1970s–1990s were assignments. You handed over the rights, and in return received an advance against future royalties. The Copyright Termination Act (17 U.S.C. §203) was specifically designed to allow songwriters to undo these assignments after a waiting period, regardless of what the contract said. The law recognizes that songwriters often had little bargaining power when they signed these deals.
A "work for hire" clause means the person who paid for the work — not the person who created it — is considered the legal author under copyright law. For music specifically: most courts have found that songs written by independent songwriters are NOT works for hire, even if the contract said so. A true work-for-hire relationship requires you to have been an actual employee of the company, not a freelance creator. This is important because works-for-hire are NOT eligible for copyright termination. If your old contract included this language, it doesn't automatically mean you're locked out — it means you should consult an entertainment attorney to evaluate your specific situation.
Copyright registration is the formal filing of your song with the U.S. Copyright Office (USCO). You receive a registration number (e.g., PA 1-234-567) as proof of ownership and the date your copyright was recorded. Registration is not required to own a copyright — you own it the moment you create the work. But it is required to file a lawsuit for infringement, and it creates the official public record that termination notices reference. Rights Back® automatically searches our database of 4.5 million+ USCO copyright registrations. Just enter your song title and we'll find the registration details for you — no manual searching required.
The Copyright Act gives songwriters (and their heirs) a one-time window to reclaim rights they transferred — even if the contract was signed as a permanent assignment. §203 — Covers works assigned on or after January 1, 1978. Termination can be exercised between 35 and 40 years after the assignment date, with a proper notice filed 2–10 years in advance. §304(c) — Covers works published before January 1, 1978, where rights were assigned under the old Copyright Act. The termination window opens 56 years after the original copyright date. §304(d) — An extended window for §304(c) works if the §304(c) window was missed. The right belongs to the songwriter (or their heirs) and CANNOT be signed away in advance. Any contract clause that says "the songwriter waives termination rights" is unenforceable.
